# Writing voice — core (all genres)

Universal rules for writing in Yesselman's voice. The genre files (papers, grants,
reviewer-responses, short-form) build on this; each one pulls this in. Built 2026-06-01
from his direct A/B phrasing choices, weighted over the published corpus.

## The voice in three words
Clear. Concise. Direct.

## Principles
- **Active voice.** "We probed RNAs with DMS," never "RNA was treated with DMS."
- **Short sentences.** Prefer several short sentences over one long one. Staccato beats sprawl: "RNA folds in two steps. First, base pairs form. Then tertiary contacts lock the structure."
- **Commit.** State conclusions assertively. "This hydrogen bond shields C5's N1, lowering its reactivity," not "may likely shield… which would lower." One claim, stated plainly. (More assertive than the published papers; intentional.)
- **Concrete, numbers inline.** Put the value right after the claim (R² = 0.68).
- **Accessible.** Gloss a technical term the first time, inline: "the [Mg2+]₁/₂, the magnesium concentration at which half the RNA is folded."
- **Lead with purpose.** Open explanatory paragraphs with the goal: "To probe how Mg2+ drives contact formation, we titrated magnesium across 98 variants."

## Banned (do not produce)
- **Dashes.** No em-dashes, and no spaced hyphens used as connectors. Use a comma, colon, period, or parentheses instead. (Strong personal dislike; ordinary compound hyphens like "high-throughput" are fine.)
- **AI-tells.** delve, leverage, moreover, furthermore as filler, "it's worth noting," "in conclusion," "plays a crucial role."
- **Hedging stacks.** Never pile qualifiers ("may possibly suggest"). At most one qualifier, and only when genuinely uncertain.
- **Passive voice and nominalizations.** "we observed," not "it was observed that"; use verbs, not "the observation of."
- **Vague filler.** No "very," no "significant" or "robust" without a number, no empty intensifiers.

## Quick test
Read it aloud. If a sentence runs out of breath, split it. If a word does no work, cut it. If a claim hides behind a qualifier, decide whether you believe it, then say so.
